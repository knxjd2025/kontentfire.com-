# Chapter 2: Groundwork -- Defining Your Brand Voice

Maria Gutierrez has been running Gutierrez Family HVAC in San Antonio for fourteen years. She started the company with her husband, a used service van, and a stack of business cards printed at Office Depot. Today she has six trucks, eleven employees, and a reputation that stretches across three counties.

Ask any of her regular customers why they keep calling Maria, and you will hear the same thing over and over. "She explains everything so you actually understand it." "She never tries to sell you something you don't need." "She treats you like family."

That right there is a brand voice. Maria just never called it that.

This chapter is about figuring out what your brand voice already is, writing it down, and then teaching AI to use it so every piece of content sounds like it came from you -- not from a robot wearing a suit.

---

## You Already Have a Brand Voice

Here is the thing most marketing people won't tell you: you don't need to invent a brand voice. You already have one. It has been developing every single day you have been in business.

It is the way you answer the phone. It is the way you explain to a homeowner why their compressor failed. It is the way you calm down a first-time customer who is worried about getting ripped off. It is the way your team talks to each other on a job site, and it is the way your best technician talks to a nervous grandmother who is afraid to turn on her furnace.

Think about the last time you sat at a customer's kitchen table. You probably didn't say, "Our proprietary diagnostic methodology enables us to identify system inefficiencies and deliver optimized thermal management solutions." You would never say that. Nobody would. You probably said something more like, "Your condenser is shot. The good news is we can swap it out tomorrow morning, and here's what it'll cost. No surprises."

That is your brand voice.

Maria's version of this goes something like: "I know this isn't how you wanted to spend your Saturday. Let me show you exactly what's going on so you can make the best decision for your family." She pulls up a photo on her phone. She draws a little diagram on the back of an invoice. She gives two options -- a repair and a replacement -- and she tells people which one she would pick if it were her house.

Her customers trust her because she talks to them like a real person. She doesn't hide behind jargon. She doesn't upsell. She makes people feel safe spending money, which is the single most powerful thing you can do in the trades.

Now here is the problem. When Maria first tried using AI to write social media posts, what came out sounded nothing like her. It sounded like it was written by a marketing intern at a Fortune 500 company. It sounded corporate. Stiff. Generic. It sounded like every other business on the internet.

She almost gave up on AI entirely. A lot of trade pros do.

But the issue was never the AI itself. The issue was that nobody told the AI how Maria talks.

---

## Teaching AI Not to Sound Like a Robot

This is the number one complaint from every trade business owner who tries AI for the first time: "It sounds fake."

And they are right. It does sound fake. Out of the box, with no guidance, AI defaults to what it has seen the most of -- corporate marketing language. The kind of language that fills annual reports and enterprise software websites. The kind of language that makes normal people's eyes glaze over.

Here is what default AI sounds like when you ask it to write a social media post for an HVAC company:

> "Our HVAC solutions leverage cutting-edge technology to ensure optimal climate control for your home. With our team of certified professionals, we deliver unparalleled comfort and energy efficiency. Contact us today to unleash the full potential of your heating and cooling system."

Read that out loud. Does that sound like any HVAC company owner you have ever met? Does that sound like something Maria would post? Does that sound like something any real human being would say to another real human being?

No. It sounds like a brochure that got fed through a corporate buzzword generator.

Now look at this version:

> "Nobody wants to wake up at 3 AM sweating in July. We fix that. Same day. No surprises on the bill."

Same service. Same company. Completely different feeling. The second version sounds like a person. It sounds like someone you would actually call. It sounds like someone you would trust.

So what changed? The AI did not get smarter between those two examples. The difference is that someone told the AI how to talk.

Here is the thing about AI that most people don't understand: it is not trying to sound corporate on purpose. It is just trying to give you something that sounds "professional" because that is what it thinks you want. You have to redirect it. You have to say, "No, don't talk like that. Talk like this."

And once you do, the results are night and day.

Let me show you exactly what I mean. Here is a basic prompt with no voice guidance:

**Prompt:** "Write a Facebook post about our HVAC tune-up special."

**AI Output:** "Ensure your HVAC system operates at peak performance this season! Our comprehensive tune-up service includes a thorough inspection, cleaning, and calibration of all critical components. Schedule your appointment today and experience the difference that professional maintenance can make. Limited-time offer -- don't miss out!"

Now here is the same request with a few lines of voice guidance added to the front:

**Prompt:** "You write social media posts for Gutierrez Family HVAC in San Antonio. You sound like a friendly neighbor, not a corporation. You use short sentences. You never use words like 'ensure,' 'comprehensive,' 'optimal,' or 'leverage.' You talk the way a real person talks. Write a Facebook post about our HVAC tune-up special."

**AI Output:** "San Antonio, your AC worked overtime this summer. It's tired. A $89 tune-up now saves you a $2,000 repair in January. We check everything, clean everything, and tell you the truth about what shape your system is in. No sales pitch. Just straight talk. Call or text Maria at 210-555-0147."

That is the same AI. The same technology. The only difference is a few sentences of instruction at the top of the prompt. That is it. That is the whole secret.

The first version talks at people. The second version talks to people. And that difference is everything when you are trying to get someone to pick up the phone and call you.

Here are some of the most common "robot words" to watch for and eliminate:

- "Ensure" -- just say "make sure" or skip it entirely
- "Leverage" -- nobody in real life leverages anything except a crowbar
- "Optimal" -- say "best" or "right"
- "Utilize" -- the word "use" works fine
- "Comprehensive" -- say "full" or "complete" or just describe what you actually do
- "Furthermore" -- you are writing a social media post, not a legal brief
- "Cutting-edge" -- say "new" or "latest" or better yet, just explain what it does
- "Unparalleled" -- say "best in town" or better yet, prove it with a story
- "Solutions" -- you don't sell solutions. You fix air conditioners.
- "Don't hesitate to" -- just say "call us" or "text us"

When you see these words in AI output, it is a dead giveaway that the AI is guessing at your voice instead of being told your voice. The fix is not to rewrite the output by hand every single time. The fix is to tell the AI who you are up front so it gets it right the first time.

## The 3 Pillars of a Blue-Collar Brand

Every strong trade business brand stands on three pillars. You don't need a marketing degree to understand them. You already live them every day. The trick is translating what you do in person into what you say online.

### Pillar 1: Trust

"I'll show up when I say I will."

Trust is the foundation of everything in the trades. Your customers are letting you into their homes. They are handing you thousands of dollars for work they can't inspect themselves. They are trusting that you won't cut corners where they can't see.

Trust is not something you claim. It is something you prove. And social media is the perfect place to prove it over and over again.

Here is how trust translates into content:

**Customer testimonials.** Not the polished, scripted kind. The real kind. A screenshot of a Google review where someone says, "Maria's team showed up on time and didn't try to sell us a new system when a repair was all we needed." That one screenshot does more than a month of promotional posts.

**Google review highlights.** Take your best reviews and turn them into social media posts. Put the customer's words front and center. Add a simple "Thank you, [name]. This is why we do what we do." Reviews are the most powerful trust content you have, and most trade businesses never share them.

**Before and after.** Show the work. Show the messy ductwork before and the clean installation after. Show the corroded wiring and the fresh replacement. People trust what they can see.

**Transparency about pricing.** Maria posts things like, "Here's what a condenser replacement actually costs and why." She breaks down the parts, the labor, the warranty. Her competitors think she is crazy for sharing that. Her customers think she is the only honest HVAC company in San Antonio.

### Pillar 2: Expertise

"I've been doing this for twenty years."

People want to hire someone who knows what they are doing. But here is the thing -- they don't want you to talk over their heads. They want you to know everything and explain it simply. That is the definition of expertise in the trades.

Here is how expertise translates into content:

**Educational tips.** "Three things to check before you call an HVAC company." "Why your upstairs is always hotter than your downstairs." "The one filter mistake that's costing you money every month." These posts position you as the expert without being preachy. They give people something useful. And when those people eventually need HVAC work, who do you think they are going to call? The company that taught them something.

**Behind-the-scenes content.** Show your team doing the work. A 30-second video of a technician explaining what they are doing during an install. A photo of a training session. A shot of your warehouse with parts organized on the shelves. This kind of content says, "We are the real deal," without saying a word.

**Myth-busting.** Every trade has myths that customers believe. "Closing vents in unused rooms saves energy." (It doesn't.) "You only need to change your filter once a year." (You absolutely don't.) Correcting these myths makes you the authority in your market.

**Certifications and training.** When someone on your team gets a new certification, post about it. When you invest in new equipment, show it off. These posts don't brag. They reassure. They say, "We take this seriously."

### Pillar 3: Relatability

"I'm your neighbor, not a corporation."

This is where trade businesses have a massive advantage over big companies, and most of them waste it. You live in the same community as your customers. You coach their kids' soccer teams. You eat at the same restaurants. You deal with the same weather, the same traffic, the same local nonsense.

Use that.

Here is how relatability translates into content:

**Team photos.** Not stiff corporate headshots. Real photos. Your crew grabbing breakfast tacos before a long day. Your apprentice on their first solo job. Your office dog sleeping on a stack of invoices. People hire people, not logos.

**Community involvement.** Sponsoring a Little League team. Donating a free furnace to a family in need. Showing up at the neighborhood block party. This content does not sell anything directly, but it builds the kind of goodwill that money cannot buy.

**Humor.** This one scares some business owners, but it shouldn't. A meme about Texas heat. A funny photo from a job site. A caption like "Monday morning, first call: 'My AC is making a sound.' Me: 'What kind of sound?' Them: 'A bad one.'" People share funny content. They don't share corporate content.

**Local flavor.** Reference local landmarks, weather, events, and culture. "San Antonio, it's gonna be 107 this weekend. If your AC has been making weird noises, now is not the time to ignore it." That one sentence connects you to your community in a way that a generic "Stay cool this summer!" never will.

When you combine all three pillars -- trust, expertise, and relatability -- you have a brand that people feel connected to. Not just a company they might call. A company they want to call. A company they recommend to their friends without being asked.

## Building Your "Brand Voice Prompt"

Alright, this is the part of the chapter where we stop talking theory and start building something you can use today. Right now. Before you finish your coffee.

A Brand Voice Prompt is a paragraph you paste into AI every time you want it to create content for your business. Think of it as a cheat sheet for the AI. It tells the AI who you are, how you talk, who your customers are, and what to avoid. Once you have it written, you never have to think about it again. You just paste it in, add your request, and the AI gives you content that actually sounds like you.

Here is the template:

> "You are a social media writer for **[Business Name]**, a **[trade]** company in **[City, State]**. We've been in business for **[X]** years. Our tone is **[friendly / professional / casual / humorous -- pick one or two]**. We talk like **[description -- e.g., 'a trusted neighbor who happens to be an expert']**. We NEVER use words like **[list corporate words to avoid]**. We ALWAYS **[specific style notes -- e.g., 'use short sentences,' 'include a call to action,' 'mention our city by name']**. Our customers are **[description -- e.g., 'homeowners in suburban San Antonio, mostly families, age 30-60']**. Our unique selling point is **[USP -- e.g., 'family-owned, same-day service, honest pricing with no hidden fees']**."

That is it. One paragraph. Maybe five minutes to fill out.

Now let me show you Maria filling this out for Gutierrez Family HVAC:

> "You are a social media writer for Gutierrez Family HVAC, a heating and air conditioning company in San Antonio, Texas. We've been in business for 14 years. Our tone is warm, friendly, and straight-talking. We talk like a trusted neighbor who happens to know everything about HVAC -- clear, honest, and never condescending. We NEVER use words like 'solutions,' 'leverage,' 'optimal,' 'ensure,' 'cutting-edge,' 'don't hesitate,' or 'unparalleled.' We ALWAYS use short sentences, mention San Antonio or the local area, include a clear call to action, and write like we're talking to a friend. Our customers are homeowners in the San Antonio metro area, mostly families, age 30 to 65, who want honest service without the runaround. Our unique selling point is that we're family-owned, offer same-day emergency service, and always give an upfront price with no hidden fees."

Now watch what happens when Maria uses this prompt versus when she doesn't.

**Without the Brand Voice Prompt:**

"Write a Facebook post about emergency AC repair."

AI output: "When your air conditioning system fails unexpectedly, our team of certified HVAC professionals is ready to restore your comfort quickly and efficiently. We offer 24/7 emergency repair services with competitive pricing and guaranteed satisfaction. Don't let a broken AC disrupt your life -- contact us today for a swift and reliable solution."

Sounds like every other HVAC company on the internet. Generic. Forgettable. Nobody is saving that post. Nobody is sharing it. Nobody is picking up the phone because of it.

**With the Brand Voice Prompt:**

"[Brand Voice Prompt from above] Write a Facebook post about emergency AC repair."

AI output: "It's 11 PM, it's 95 degrees, and your AC just quit. We've all been there. Here's the good news: we answer our phone at 11 PM. And at 2 AM. And at 5 AM on a Sunday. Maria or one of our guys will be at your door the same night. We'll tell you what's wrong, what it costs, and fix it right there. No 'we'll send someone Monday' nonsense. Save our number: 210-555-0147. You'll be glad you did when July hits."

That sounds like Maria. It sounds real. It sounds like someone who actually fixes air conditioners and actually cares about the people sweating in their living rooms. It mentions San Antonio, it mentions her name, it has a phone number, and it reads like a human being wrote it.

The difference between those two outputs is one paragraph. One paragraph pasted at the top of a prompt. That is it.

And here is the beautiful part: once you write your Brand Voice Prompt, you are done. You have it forever. You paste it in every time and the AI knows exactly how to talk for you. You might tweak it here and there as your business evolves, but the heavy lifting is done.

The manual way works great. You will paste your Brand Voice Prompt into ChatGPT every single time you want to create content. Some tools, like KontentFire, let you save your Brand Voice once and it automatically applies to everything you generate -- but the important thing right now is getting your voice defined.

## Brand Voice Formulation Template

Grab a pen or open a notes app. Fill in every blank. Don't overthink it. First instinct is usually the right one.

**My business name is:** _______________

**We are a** _______________ **company in** _______________.

**We have been in business for** _______________ **years.**

**If I had to describe how we talk to customers, I would say we sound like:** _______________

*(Examples: "a friendly uncle who knows his stuff," "the straight-talking contractor who tells it like it is," "the calm, patient teacher who never makes you feel dumb for asking questions")*

**Three words that describe our personality:**

1. _______________
2. _______________
3. _______________

*(Examples: honest, warm, no-nonsense, funny, down-to-earth, professional, dependable, energetic)*

**Words we NEVER use (circle or list all that apply):**

solutions / leverage / ensure / optimal / utilize / comprehensive / cutting-edge / unparalleled / furthermore / innovative / synergy / don't hesitate / state-of-the-art / premier

**Others to add:** _______________

**Words and phrases we ALWAYS use or like:**

*(Examples: "straight talk," "no surprises," "your neighbors," city name, team members' first names, local landmarks)*

_______________

**Our customers are:**

*(Who are they? Age range? Homeowners or businesses? What neighborhood or area? What do they care about most?)*

_______________

**The one thing that makes us different from our competitors:**

_______________

**If a customer had to describe us in one sentence, they would say:**

_______________

**Now, assemble your Brand Voice Prompt using this formula:**

> "You are a social media writer for **[Business Name]**, a **[trade]** company in **[City, State]**. We've been in business for **[X]** years. Our tone is **[your three personality words]**. We talk like **[your description from above]**. We NEVER use words like **[your never-use list]**. We ALWAYS **[your always-use notes]**. Our customers are **[your customer description]**. Our unique selling point is **[your differentiator]**."

Write it out. Read it out loud. Does it sound like you? Good. Save it somewhere you won't lose it. You are going to use it a lot.

## The Hard Way vs. The Smart Way

**The Hard Way:** You sit down every week and try to come up with social media content from scratch. You stare at a blank screen. You type something, delete it, type something else. Eventually you post something mediocre because you ran out of time. Or you skip posting entirely because you have real work to do. Weeks go by. Your social media goes quiet. Your competitors who post regularly keep getting calls from people who found them online. You wonder why your phone isn't ringing as much as it used to.

**The Smart Way:** You spend fifteen minutes -- once -- filling out the Brand Voice Formulation Template. You assemble your Brand Voice Prompt. From that point forward, every time you need content, you paste the prompt, tell the AI what you need, and you have a post that sounds like you in thirty seconds. You can create a whole week of content in the time it takes to drink a cup of coffee. Your social media stays active. Your name stays in front of people. Your phone keeps ringing.

The difference between the Hard Way and the Smart Way is not talent. It is not budget. It is not having a marketing team. It is having a system. Your Brand Voice Prompt is the first piece of that system.
