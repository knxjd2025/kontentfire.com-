## The Anatomy of a Scroll-Stopping Post

Every great social media post has three parts. Think of it like framing a house. You need the foundation, the walls, and the roof. Skip any one of those, and the whole thing falls apart.

In content terms, those three parts are:

1. The Hook
2. The Story
3. The CTA (Call to Action)

That is it. Three pieces. Master these three things, and you will write better posts than ninety percent of your competitors without breaking a sweat.

Let us break each one down.


### Part One: The Hook

The hook is the first one or two lines of your post. On most platforms, this is all people see before they have to tap "read more" or keep scrolling.

This is your make-or-break moment.

Think about how you use social media. You are lying on the couch after a long day, thumb flicking through posts at the speed of light. What makes you stop? Something surprising. Something that makes you curious. Something that hits you right in the gut.

That is what your hook needs to do.

Here is Jake's old hook: "New roof."

Here is a better hook: "This roof was 30 minutes away from collapsing into the kids' bedroom."

See the difference? The first one is a statement nobody cares about. The second one makes you stop scrolling because you need to know what happened.

Let me give you ten hook formulas that work specifically for trade service businesses. These are fill-in-the-blank templates you can use over and over again.


**1. The Shock Hook**

"We found [something alarming] during a routine inspection..."

Example: "We found three layers of shingles stacked on top of each other during a routine inspection. The decking underneath was rotting through."

This works because people love discovering hidden problems. It triggers that "oh no, could this be MY house?" feeling.


**2. The Question Hook**

"Think your [thing] is fine? Here is what we found under the [surface]..."

Example: "Think your roof is fine? Here is what we found under the shingles of a house that passed inspection two years ago."

Questions force the brain to engage. The reader cannot help but start thinking about their own situation.


**3. The Number Hook**

"[Number] signs your [thing] will not survive another [time period]"

Example: "3 signs your roof will not survive another winter."

Numbers are specific. Specific is interesting. "Some signs" is boring. "3 signs" makes people want to know all three.


**4. The Story Hook**

"Got a call at [time] from a homeowner in [emotional state]..."

Example: "Got a call at 6 AM from a homeowner in a panic. Water was pouring through her ceiling and her insurance had just lapsed."

Stories are magnetic. When you start with a real human moment, people lean in.


**5. The Myth-Buster Hook**

"Stop believing this lie about [topic]..."

Example: "Stop believing this lie about metal roofs. Your neighbor who told you they are too loud in the rain? Dead wrong."

People love being told they have been lied to. It is controversial without being offensive.


**6. The Before/After Hook**

"Swipe to see what [time period] of neglect looks like vs. what we did in [time period]."

Example: "Swipe to see what 15 years of neglect looks like vs. what we did in two days."

The contrast creates curiosity. People want to see the transformation.


**7. The Money Hook**

"Here is why your [provider's] cheapest quote might cost you $[big number]."

Example: "Here is why your roofer's cheapest quote might cost you $20,000."

Money gets attention. Especially big, scary numbers tied to something the reader might be doing wrong.


**8. The Confession Hook**

"I am going to be honest about something most [trade] companies will not tell you..."

Example: "I am going to be honest about something most roofing companies will not tell you. You probably do not need a full replacement."

Honesty is disarming. When a business admits something that might cost them a sale, trust skyrockets.


**9. The "I Wish" Hook**

"I wish this homeowner had called us [time period] ago..."

Example: "I wish this homeowner had called us six months ago. We could have saved them $8,000."

Regret is a powerful motivator. This hook makes people think, "Am I waiting too long?"


**10. The Behind-the-Scenes Hook**

"Here is what actually happens on a [project] that nobody shows you..."

Example: "Here is what actually happens on a roof tear-off that nobody shows you. It is not pretty, but it is why we charge what we charge."

People are curious about processes. Showing the messy reality builds respect for the work.


### Part Two: The Story

The story is the body of your post. It is where you deliver on the promise your hook made.

Here is the most important rule: write like you are texting a friend, not writing an essay.

Forget everything your English teacher told you about proper paragraphs. On social media, short paragraphs win. One thought per paragraph. Use line breaks between ideas. Keep sentences simple and direct.

Here is what this looks like in practice.

Bad (wall of text):
"We just finished this beautiful roof replacement in the Ballantyne area of Charlotte. The homeowners had been dealing with leaks for months and had already gotten three quotes from other companies. When we came out to do the inspection we found that the previous roofers had actually installed the flashing incorrectly which was causing water to pool near the chimney. We tore off the old shingles, replaced the damaged decking, installed new ice and water shield, and put on GAF Timberline HDZ shingles in Charcoal. The whole project took two days and the homeowners were thrilled with the result."

That is one giant block of text. On a phone screen, it looks like a homework assignment. Nobody is reading that.

Good (broken up and conversational):
"Just wrapped up this one in Ballantyne, and I have to tell you about it.

The homeowners had been dealing with leaks for months. Three quotes from other companies. Nobody could figure out where the water was coming from.

We got up there and found it in about ten minutes.

The previous roofers installed the flashing wrong around the chimney. Water had been pooling there for who knows how long.

We tore everything off. Replaced the damaged decking. Laid down new ice and water shield. Finished with GAF Timberline HDZ in Charcoal.

Two days. Done.

The look on their faces when they saw the finished product? That is why we do this."

Same information. Completely different reading experience. The second version feels like a real person talking to you. The first version feels like a contractor's report.

A few rules for your story section:

**Use real details.** Do not say "a customer in the area." Say "a homeowner in Ballantyne." Do not say "we fixed the problem." Say "we replaced 14 sheets of rotting decking." Specific details make stories believable.

**Include the problem and the solution.** Every good story has tension. What was wrong? What did you do about it? What was the result? Problem, solution, outcome.

**Show your expertise without bragging.** Instead of saying "we are the best roofers in Charlotte," say "we got up there and found the issue in about ten minutes." Let the reader conclude you are good at what you do. That is way more powerful than telling them.

**Keep it under 150 words for most platforms.** Facebook gives you more room. Instagram and TikTok captions should be tighter. Google Business Profile updates should be short and punchy. Adjust your length to the platform, but when in doubt, shorter wins.


### Part Three: The CTA (Call to Action)

The call to action is the last part of your post. It tells people what to do next.

Here is the mistake most trade businesses make: they go straight for the hard sell.

"CALL NOW FOR A FREE ESTIMATE!!! 555-555-5555"

That is the social media equivalent of a guy at a party who walks up to a stranger and immediately tries to sell them insurance. Nobody wants that.

Soft CTAs work dramatically better on social media. A soft CTA invites engagement without pressure. It feels like a conversation, not a sales pitch.

Here are some soft CTAs that work:

- "Drop a comment if you have seen this before."
- "Save this for later. You will need it."
- "DM us if you want us to take a look at yours."
- "Tag a homeowner who needs to see this."
- "Have questions? Drop them below. We answer everything."
- "Want a free inspection? Shoot us a message."

Notice how these feel different from "CALL NOW!!!" They invite people in instead of pushing them toward a sale.

The psychology is simple. On social media, people are relaxing. They are not in buying mode. A soft CTA respects that. It says, "Hey, we are here when you are ready." And when they ARE ready, guess whose name pops into their head first?

Yours.


## Prompting AI for Captions That Convert

Now that you understand the three-part structure, let us talk about how to get AI to write these posts for you.

The universal caption prompt is your Swiss Army knife. Memorize this formula, and you can generate a solid caption for any platform, any time, in about thirty seconds.

Here is the template:

> "Write a [platform] post for my [trade] business. Use this photo context: [describe what is in the photo]. Start with a hook that stops the scroll. Keep the tone [friendly/casual/expert]. Include a soft call to action at the end. Keep it under [word count] words. Use my brand voice: [paste brand voice prompt from Chapter 3]."

That is it. One prompt. Works every time. Let me show you five real examples with the prompt AND the AI output.


### Example 1: Before/After Roof Replacement

**The Prompt:**

"Write a Facebook post for my roofing business. Use this photo context: a side-by-side before and after of a roof replacement. The before shows old, curling, moss-covered shingles on a ranch-style home. The after shows brand-new dark gray architectural shingles. Start with a hook that stops the scroll. Keep the tone friendly and proud. Include a soft call to action at the end. Keep it under 120 words. Use my brand voice: We are Jake's Roofing, a family-owned roofing company in Charlotte, NC. We talk like a trusted neighbor, not a salesman. Straightforward, honest, and a little proud of our work."

**The AI Output:**

"15 years of wear on the left. Two days of our crew on the right.

This ranch home in Matthews had shingles that were curling so bad you could see daylight through the gaps. Moss was eating through the granules. One more bad storm and the homeowners would have been dealing with a lot more than a roof bill.

We stripped it down to the deck, replaced a few soft spots, and laid down new GAF Timberline HDZ in Charcoal.

Night and day difference.

If your roof is starting to look like that before photo, do not wait for the leak. Shoot us a DM and we will come take a look. No pressure, no sales pitch. Just honest answers."


### Example 2: Team Photo on a Job Site

**The Prompt:**

"Write an Instagram post for my roofing business. Use this photo context: my crew of six guys standing on a finished roof at sunset, smiling and covered in dust. Start with a hook that shows appreciation for the team. Keep the tone genuine and proud. Include a soft call to action at the end. Keep it under 100 words."

**The AI Output:**

"This is the crew that makes it happen.

Six guys. Up before dawn. On the roof in the heat, the cold, the rain. They do not complain. They just get it done.

Today we wrapped up a full tear-off and replacement in Huntersville. 28 squares. One day.

I do not post enough about the people behind the work, so here it is. These guys are the reason we have a five-star reputation.

Know someone who needs a roof they can count on? Tag them below."


### Example 3: Seasonal Maintenance Tip

**The Prompt:**

"Write a Facebook post for my roofing business. Use this photo context: a close-up of leaves and debris clogged in a gutter on a residential home in fall. Start with a helpful warning hook. Keep the tone knowledgeable but casual. Include a soft call to action. Keep it under 100 words."

**The AI Output:**

"Your gutters right now probably look exactly like this. And that is a problem.

When leaves and debris clog your gutters, water backs up under your shingles. That is how leaks start. That is how fascia boards rot. That is how a $200 gutter cleaning turns into a $5,000 repair.

Before winter hits, get up there and clear them out. Or better yet, have someone do it for you.

We offer gutter cleanings and can check your roof while we are up there. Drop us a message if you want to get on the schedule."

Use this photo context: a screenshot of a five-star Google review from a customer named Maria who said we were professional, on time, and cleaned up perfectly. Start with a grateful hook. Keep the tone humble and appreciative. Include a soft call to action. Keep it under 100 words."

**The AI Output:**

### Example 5: Behind-the-Scenes Moment

**The Prompt:**

"Write a TikTok caption for my roofing business. Use this photo context: a short video of me doing a roof inspection, walking across a roof and pointing out damaged areas to the homeowner who is watching from the ground. Start with a curiosity hook. Keep the tone real and down-to-earth. Include a soft call to action. Keep it under 80 words."

But whether you use a template or write your own prompts, the formula stays the same. Describe what is in the photo. Tell the AI who you are. Ask for a hook. Ask for a CTA. Set the tone. Set the length.

Do that, and you will get usable content ninety percent of the time.

## Formatting for the Human Eye

You can write the most brilliant caption in the world, but if it looks like a wall of text on someone's phone, they are not going to read it.

Formatting is not an afterthought. It is half the battle.

Here are the rules.


### Short Paragraphs

Two to three sentences maximum. On a phone screen, a three-sentence paragraph already looks long. When in doubt, hit enter.


### Line Breaks Between Thoughts

Every time you shift to a new idea, add a blank line. This creates visual breathing room. It makes your post feel fast and easy to read, even if it is two hundred words long.


### Strategic Emoji Use

Emojis are tools, not decorations. Use one to three per post. Pick relevant ones. A house emoji. A tools emoji. A checkmark. A pointing finger to draw attention to your CTA.

Do NOT do this: "We just finished this AMAZING roof!!!! So BLESSED to work with such an INCREDIBLE team!!!"

That reads like a parody account. One or two emojis, placed with purpose.


### Bullet Points for Lists

When you are listing tips, signs, or steps, use bullet points. They are easier to scan than a paragraph, and scanning is how most people read social media.


### ALL CAPS Sparingly

You can use all caps on ONE word for emphasis. "We found MOLD under three layers of shingles." That is effective. "WE FOUND MOLD UNDER THREE LAYERS OF SHINGLES" feels like yelling.


### Hashtag Strategy

Use five to ten targeted hashtags. Make them a mix of:
- Your location: #CharlotteRoofer #MyersPark #CLTContractor
- Your trade: #RoofReplacement #MetalRoofing #RoofingLife
- Your niche: #StandingSeamMetal #CedarShakeReplacement
- Community: #CharlotteNC #CLTSmallBusiness

Do NOT use thirty generic hashtags like #love #instagood #photooftheday. Those attract bots, not customers.


### Bad Format vs. Good Format: Side by Side

Here is the same content formatted two different ways.

**Bad Format:**

"We just finished a roof replacement in Ballantyne today and wanted to share the results with you all! The homeowners had an old three-tab shingle roof that was over 20 years old and leaking in multiple spots. We came out and did a full inspection and found that the decking had water damage in several areas. Our crew tore off the old roof, replaced the damaged decking, installed new underlayment and put on brand new architectural shingles. The whole project took about a day and a half and the homeowners are really happy with how it turned out. If you need a roof replacement give us a call at 555-555-5555! #roofing #roofer #roof #newroof #homeimprovement #contractor #charlottenc #ballantyne #shingles #construction #renovation #remodel #fixerupper #beforeandafter #qualitywork #roofinglife #roofersofinstagram #smallbusiness #familyowned #fivestarreviews #trustedcontractor #topcontractor #bestofcharlotte #clt #queencity #nccontractor #ncroofing"

Brutal. One giant paragraph. A hard-sell CTA. Twenty-seven hashtags. Nobody is reading this. Nobody.

**Good Format:**

"This Ballantyne home had a 20-year-old roof that was leaking in three spots.

The homeowners thought they needed a patch job. Turns out, the decking underneath had water damage that was spreading fast.

Here is what we did:
- Full tear-off of the old three-tab shingles
- Replaced damaged decking in four sections
- New synthetic underlayment
- GAF Timberline HDZ architectural shingles

Day and a half. Done right.

The homeowners said they wish they had called us sooner. We hear that a lot.

Thinking about your roof? DM us. Free inspections, honest answers.

#CharlotteRoofer #Ballantyne #RoofReplacement #ArchitecturalShingles #CLTContractor #NCRoofing #RoofingDoneRight"

Same project. Completely different impact. The second version gets read. The first version gets scrolled past.
